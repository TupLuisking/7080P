Who We Are

The 70/80 Project is a promotional and educational design initiative which aims to foster greater awareness for Filipino films made during the 1970s and 1980s.

70/80P is meant for aspiring young filmmakers and cinephiles who have no idea how to access films from this era - or simply where to start.

Accessibility is only the beginning. 70/80P wishes to tell the stories of the directors behind these films, with the ultimate goal being to inspire a new generation of Filipino filmmakers with the classic techniques of the past.

The project has three main avenues: (1) the 70/80P website (you’re here right now, hello there!) with information on the films, directors, and screenings; (2) modules and memorabilia, which are free, educational, well-researched, and made for three films each month; and (3) film screenings, which correspond to our MiniFests. (We are currently looking for a new home for our screenings - hit us up if you’re interested!) The project is completely non-profit and entirely self-funded. If you find any joy or value in what we do, please consider donating or becoming a member (details for this below).

Frequently Asked Questions

How did it start?

The 70/80 Project started out as a design thesis in March 2018. It was born out of a love for film, a love for design, and a determination to fix what we saw as an urgent problem for Philippine cinema. We also wanted to make the answer to the question, “What would we have wanted as a freshman in college?”

How does the project work?

First, we scout on what films are being shown for the month. After checking what’s logistically convenient, we curate and choose 3 films based on a theme. Promotional and educational materials are made for these 3 films based on academic research and design theory. Lastly, we promote a heck ton out of the films we’ve chosen (they deserve it!) and distribute the materials at screenings.

Why 70s and 80s?

The second golden age of Philippine cinema, which happened during the 1970s and 1980s, put the spotlight on filmmakers themselves. “What are you trying to say with this film?” was a question directors tried to answer. A time of artistic integrity and rebellious cinematic experimentation in the midst of tyranny and dictatorship, these were our first films to actually confront the nation’s problems. This culture also fostered a national audience which became more engaged in informed discussion and analysis.
