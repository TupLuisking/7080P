Marilou Diaz-Abaya started off her career with a series of feminist lms. Her anger towards systematic oppression resulted in her being one of the few filmmakers who actually showed female narratives during the time of Marcos regime.

It should be noted that even in the international film scene, it was very rare for a female director to be as prolific as MDA was in the 1980s.

After establishing herself as a director for women, she eventually began to cover lms that had to do with Philippine history, a subject she had been obsessed with ever since childhood.

THE
PRODUCTION

!! = point of interest

FT = notable film technique

PRE-PROD

FT: film revolves around and gives equal attention to the storylines of four different characters whose lives intersect (!! unusual for its time, which is why audiences could not connect right away)

Inseparability from current events: a lot of the characters’ struggles are inspired by daily life situations during the Martial Law era, such as student activism

PROD

No big-name stars, Lorna Tolentino was just starting out

FT: Spot the complex mise-en-scene, meticulous production design, and sensitive camera work - Did this add to the realness of the characters? Did you feel like an observer, or like you were part of the story itself?

Abaya quote: “While making it, I was already feeling very anxious and uncomfortable about the fact that I thought this is too personal. Baka ako lang ang makakaintindi nito. (I might be the only one to understand it.) I was really hurt when I felt it was rejected. Even if you anticipated that rejection.”

POST-PROD

!!: The editing and cinematography were both done by the same person, Manolo Abaya, who was MDA’s husband up until her death. He actually made her interested in film when she was 15. He ended up doing most of the cinematography of her body of work.

FT: The film was edited such that each of the four storylines was given equal importance

THE
REACTION

Initial

Moral went relatively under the radar. Unlike its successor and predecessor, it was entered into MMFF but the critics were not used to the format of multiple intersecting storylines

However, it has since grown a following of its own, being chosen to be remastered by the ABS-CBN Restoration Project.

Present

1984 British Film Institute Outstanding Film of the Year

!!: MDA received this award from London, the city where she trained to be a filmmaker

On Film

One of the interesting aspects of local cinema is that Filipinos have never had a problem with featuring female directors and themes. Joyce Bernal, Malou Santos, Olivia Lamasan, and Cathy Molina-Garcia are some of the other female filmmakers who dominate local cinema today, and many women in the business cite MDA as one of their heroes.
